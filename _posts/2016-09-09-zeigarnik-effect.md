In [psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology "Psychology"), the **Zeigarnik effect** states that people remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed tasks. In [Gestalt psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestalt_psychology "Gestalt psychology"), the Zeigarnik effect has been used to demonstrate the general presence of Gestalt phenomena: not just appearing as perceptual effects, but also present in cognition.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-1)

Overview
--------

Russian psychologist [Bluma Zeigarnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bluma_Zeigarnik "Bluma Zeigarnik") first studied the phenomenon after her professor, [Gestalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestalt_psychology "Gestalt psychology") psychologist [Kurt Lewin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Lewin "Kurt Lewin"), noticed that a [waiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waiter "Waiter") had better recollections of still unpaid orders. However, after the completion of the task – after everyone had paid – he was unable to remember any more details of the orders. Zeigarnik then designed a series of experiments to uncover the processes underlying this phenomenon. Her research report was published in 1927.[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-2)

The advantage of remembrance can be explained by looking at [Lewin's field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_theory_(psychology) "Field theory (psychology)"): a task that has already been started establishes a task-specific tension, which improves cognitive accessibility of the relevant contents.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-Lewin1935-3) The tension is relieved upon completion of the task. If the task is interrupted, the reduction of tension is impeded. Through continuous tension, the content is made more easily accessible, and can be easily remembered.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-Lewin1935-3)

The Zeigarnik effect suggests that students who suspend their study to perform unrelated activities (such as studying a different subject or playing a game), will remember material better than students who complete study sessions without a break (McKinney 1935; Zeigarnik 1927).

### Ovsiankina effect

The Zeigarnik effect should not be confused with the [Ovsiankina effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovsiankina_effect "Ovsiankina effect"). [Maria Ovsiankina](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Maria_Ovsiankina&action=edit&redlink=1 "Maria Ovsiankina (page does not exist)") was a colleague of Bluma Zeigarnik who investigated the effect of task interruption on the tendency to resume the task at the next opportunity.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-4)

### Harden rule

The Zeigarnik effect has been used to explain the widespread criticism of the [National Basketball Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Basketball_Association "National Basketball Association") in allowing [free throws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_throws "Free throws") for a player "chucking it up whenever a guy comes near them." There is a stoppage of play with each foul. When repeatedly done, it is felt to build up a [cognitive bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias "Cognitive bias") against this move. The criticism necessitated a rule change penalizing this activity, known as the Harden Rule, named after its most prominent user, [James Harden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Harden "James Harden").[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-5)[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-6)[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-7)

Criticism
---------

The reliability of the effect has been a matter of some controversy.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeigarnik_effect#cite_note-Einstein2003-8)

Several later studies, performed later in other countries, attempting to replicate Zeigarnik's experiment, failed to find any significant differences in recall between "finished" and "unfinished" (interrupted) tasks (e.g. Van Bergen, 1968).
