_**Zugzwang**_ (German for "compulsion to move", pronounced [\[ˈtsuːktsvaŋ\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German")) is a situation found in [chess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess "Chess") and other games wherein one [player](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Player_(game) "Player (game)") is put at a disadvantage because they must make a move when they would prefer to pass and not move. The fact that the player is compelled to move means that their position will become significantly weaker. A player is said to be "in zugzwang" when any possible move will worsen their position.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-1)

Although the term is used less precisely in games such as chess, it is used specifically in [combinatorial game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_game_theory "Combinatorial game theory") to denote a move that directly changes the outcome of the game from a win to a loss.[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-2)[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-3) Putting the opponent in zugzwang is a common way to help the superior side win a game, and in some cases, it is necessary in order to make the win possible.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-mull08p173-4)

The term _zugzwang_ was used in German chess literature in 1858 or earlier,[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-winter97-5) and the first known use of the term in English was by [World Champion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Chess_Championship "World Chess Championship") [Emanuel Lasker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker "Emanuel Lasker") in 1905.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-winter08-6) The concept of zugzwang was known to chess players many [centuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centuries "Centuries") before the term was coined, appearing in an [endgame study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endgame_study "Endgame study") published in 1604 by [Alessandro Salvio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alessandro_Salvio "Alessandro Salvio"), one of the first writers on the game, and in [shatranj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shatranj "Shatranj") studies dating back to the early 9th century, over 1000 years before the first known use of the term.

Positions with zugzwang occur fairly often in chess [endgames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess_endgame "Chess endgame"), especially in [king and pawn endgames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_and_pawn_versus_king_endgame "King and pawn versus king endgame"). According to [John Nunn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Nunn "John Nunn"), positions of reciprocal zugzwang are surprisingly important in the analysis of endgames.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-nunn95p6-7)[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-nunn99p7-8)

  

Etymology
---------

The word comes from German _Zug_ 'move' + _Zwang_ 'compulsion', so that _Zugzwang_ means 'being forced to make a move'. Originally the term was used interchangeably with the term _zugpflicht_ 'obligation to make a move' as a general game rule. Games like chess and [checkers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkers "Checkers") have "zugzwang" (or "zugpflicht"): a player _must_ always make a move on his turn even if this is to their disadvantage. Over time, the term became especially associated with chess.

According to chess [historian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historian "Historian") [Edward Winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Winter_(chess_historian) "Edward Winter (chess historian)"), the term had been in use in German chess circles in the 19th century.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-winter97-5)

> Pages 353–358 of the September 1858 Deutsche Schachzeitung had an unsigned article "Zugzwang, Zugwahl und Privilegien". [Friedrich Amelung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Amelung "Friedrich Amelung") employed the terms Zugzwang, Tempozwang and Tempozugzwang on pages 257–259 of the September 1896 issue of the same magazine. When a perceived example of zugzwang occurred in the third game of the 1896–97 world championship match between Steinitz and Lasker, after 34...Rg8, the Deutsche Schachzeitung (December 1896, page 368) reported that "White has died of zugzwang".

The earliest known use of the term _zugzwang_ in English was on page 166 of the February 1905 issue of _[Lasker's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker "Emanuel Lasker") Chess Magazine_.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-winter08-6) The term did not become common in English-language chess sources until the 1930s, after the publication of the English translation of Nimzowitsch's _[My System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/My_System "My System")_ in 1929.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-winter97-5)

History
-------

The concept of zugzwang, if not the term, must have been known to players for many centuries. Zugzwang is required to win the elementary (and common) [king and rook versus king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkmate#King_and_rook "Checkmate") [endgame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endgame_(chess) "Endgame (chess)"),[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-9) and the king and rook (or differently-named pieces with the same powers) have been chess pieces since the earliest versions of the game.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-10)

[Joseph Bertin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bertin "Joseph Bertin") refers to zugzwang in _The Noble Game of Chess_ (1735), wherein he documents 19 rules about chess play. His 18th rule is: "To play well the latter end of a game, you must calculate who has the move, on which the game always depends."[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-14)

[François-André Danican Philidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Andr%C3%A9_Danican_Philidor "François-André Danican Philidor") wrote in 1777 of the position illustrated that after White plays 36.Kc3, Black "is obliged to move his rook from his king, which gives you an opportunity of taking his rook by a double check \[sic\], or making him [mate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkmate "Checkmate")".[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-15) Lasker explicitly cited a mirror image of this position (White: king on f3, queen on h4; Black: king on g1, rook on g2) as an example of zugzwang in _Lasker's Manual of Chess_.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-16) The British master [George Walker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Walker_(chess_player) "George Walker (chess player)") analyzed a similar position in the same endgame, giving a maneuver ([triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(chess) "Triangulation (chess)")) that resulted in the superior side reaching the initial position, but now with the inferior side on move and in zugzwang. Walker wrote of the superior side's decisive move: "throwing the move upon Black, in the initial position, and thereby winning".[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-17)

Zugzwang in chess
-----------------

There are three types of chess positions:

1.  each side would benefit if it were their turn to move
2.  only one player would be at a disadvantage if it were their turn to move
3.  both players would be at a disadvantage if it were their turn to move.

The great majority of positions are of the first type. In chess literature, most writers call positions of the second type _zugzwang_, and the third type _reciprocal zugzwang_ or _mutual zugzwang_. Some writers call the second type a _squeeze_ and the third type _zugzwang_.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-19)

Normally in chess, having [tempo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tempo_(chess) "Tempo (chess)") is desirable because the player who is to move has the advantage of being able to choose a move that improves their situation. Zugzwang typically occurs when "the player to move cannot do anything without making an important concession".[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-20)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-21)

Zugzwang most often occurs in the endgame when the number of pieces, and so the number of possible moves, is reduced, and the exact move chosen is often critical. The first diagram shows the simplest possible example of zugzwang. If it is White's move, they must either [stalemate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalemate "Stalemate") Black with 1.Kc6 or abandon the [pawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pawn_(chess) "Pawn (chess)"), allowing 1...Kxc7 with a draw. If it is Black's move, the only legal move is 1...Kb7, which allows White to win with 2.Kd7 followed by [queening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promotion_(chess) "Promotion (chess)") the pawn on the next move.

The second diagram is another simple example. Black, on move, must allow White to play Kc5 or Ke5, when White wins one or more [pawns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pawn_(chess) "Pawn (chess)") and can advance his own pawn toward [promotion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promotion_(chess) "Promotion (chess)"). White, on move, must retreat his [king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_(chess) "King (chess)"), when Black is out of danger.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-flear04p11-22) The squares d4 and d6 are _[corresponding squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corresponding_squares "Corresponding squares")_. Whenever the white king is on d4 with White to move, the black king must be on d6 to prevent the advance of the white king.

In many cases, the player having the move can put the other player in zugzwang by using _[triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(chess) "Triangulation (chess)")_. This often occurs in king and pawn endgames. Pieces other than the king can also triangulate to achieve zugzwang, such as in the [Philidor position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philidor_position "Philidor position"). Zugzwang is a mainstay of [chess compositions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess_composition "Chess composition") and occurs frequently in [endgame studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endgame_study "Endgame study").

Reciprocal zugzwang, White to move draws, Black to move loses.

A special case of zugzwang is _reciprocal zugzwang_ or _mutual zugzwang_, which is a position such that whoever is to move is in zugzwang. Studying positions of reciprocal zugzwang is in the analysis of endgames.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-nunn95p6-7)[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-nunn99p7-8) A position of mutual zugzwang is closely related to a game with a Conway value of zero in [game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_game_theory "Combinatorial game theory").[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-33)

In a position with reciprocal zugzwang, only the player to move is actually in zugzwang. However, the player who is not in zugzwang must play carefully because one inaccurate move can cause him to be put in zugzwang.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-34) That is in contrast to regular zugzwang, because the superior side usually has a [waiting move](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waiting_move "Waiting move") to put the opponent in zugzwang.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-nunn99p7-8)

The diagram on the right shows a position of reciprocal zugzwang. If Black is to move, 1... Kd7 is forced, which loses because White will move 2. Kb7, promote the pawn, and win. If White is to move the result is a draw as White must either stalemate Black with 1. Kc6 or allow Black to [capture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#capture "Glossary of chess") the pawn. Since each side would be in zugzwang if it were his move, it is a reciprocal zugzwang.[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-35)[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-36)

Zugzwang in the middlegame and complex endgames
-----------------------------------------------

Alex Angos notes that, "As the number of pieces on the board increases, the probability for _zugzwang_ to occur decreases."[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-42) As such, zugzwang is very rarely seen in the [middlegame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middlegame_(chess) "Middlegame (chess)").[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-43)

White has a few pawn moves which do not lose material, but eventually he will have to move one of his pieces. If he plays 1.Rc1 or Rd1, then 1...Re2 traps White's queen; 1.Kh2 fails to 1...R5f3, also trapping the queen, since White cannot play 2.Bxf3 because the bishop is [pinned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pin_(chess) "Pin (chess)") to the king; 1.g4 runs into 1...R5f3 2.Bxf3? Rh2 mate. Angos analyzes 1.a3 a5 2.axb4 axb4 3.h4 Kh8 (waiting) 4.b3 Kg8 and White has run out of waiting moves and must lose material. Best in this line is 5.Nc3!? bxc3 6.bxc3, which just leaves Black with a serious positional advantage and an extra pawn.[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-46) Other moves lose material in more obvious ways.

However, since Black would win even without the zugzwang,[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-47) it is debatable whether the position is true zugzwang. Even if White could pass his move he would still lose, albeit more slowly, after 1...R5f3 2.Bxf3 Rxf3, trapping the queen and thus winning queen and bishop for two rooks.[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-48)[Wolfgang Heidenfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Heidenfeld "Wolfgang Heidenfeld") thus considers it a misnomer to call this a true zugzwang position.[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-49) See also [Immortal Zugzwang Game: Objections to the sobriquet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immortal_Zugzwang_Game#Objections_to_the_sobriquet "Immortal Zugzwang Game").

This game between [Wilhelm Steinitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Steinitz "Wilhelm Steinitz") versus [Emanuel Lasker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker "Emanuel Lasker") in the 1896–97 [World Chess Championship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Chess_Championship "World Chess Championship"),[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-50) is an early example of zugzwang in the middlegame. After Lasker's 34...Re8–g8!, Steinitz had no [playable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#playable "Glossary of chess") moves, and [resigned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resign_(chess) "Resign (chess)").[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-Reinfeld_1965_71-51)[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-52)[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-53)[\[54\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-54) White's bishop cannot move because that would allow the crushing ...Rg2+. The queen cannot move without abandoning either its defense of the bishop on g5 or of the g2 square, where it is preventing ...Qg2#. White's move 35.f6 loses the bishop: 35...Rxg5 36. f7 Rg2+, forcing [mate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkmate "Checkmate"). The move 35.Kg1 allows 35...Qh1+ 36.Kf2 Qg2+ followed by [capturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capture_(chess) "Capture (chess)") the bishop. The rook cannot leave the first [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#rank "Glossary of chess"), as that would allow 35...Qh1#. Rook moves along the first rank other than 35.Rg1 allow 35...Qxf5, when 36.Bxh4 is impossible because of 36...Rg2+; for example, 35.Rd1 Qxf5 36.d5 Bd7, winning. That leaves only 35.Rg1, when Black wins with 35...Rxg5! 36.Qxg5 (36.Rxg5? Qh1#) Qd6+ 37.Rg3 hxg3+ 38.Qxg3 Be8 39.h4 Qxg3+ 40.Kxg3 b5! 41.axb5 a4! and Black [queens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promotion_(chess) "Promotion (chess)") first.[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-Reinfeld_1965_71-51) Colin Crouch calls the final position, "An even more perfect middlegame zugzwang than ... Sämisch–Nimzowitsch ... in the final position Black has no direct threats, and no clear plan to improve the already excellent positioning of his pieces, and yet any move by White loses instantly".[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-55)

Position after 9...axb5. According to Rowson, White is in _Zugzwang Lite_.

[Jonathan Rowson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Rowson "Jonathan Rowson") coined the term _Zugzwang Lite_ to describe a situation, sometimes arising in symmetrical [opening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess_opening "Chess opening") variations, where White's "extra move" is a burden.[\[61\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-61) He cites as an example of this phenomenon in [Hodgson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Hodgson "Julian Hodgson") versus [Arkell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keith_Arkell "Keith Arkell") at [Newcastle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne "Newcastle upon Tyne") 2001. The position at left arose after **1\. c4 c5 2. g3 g6 3. Bg2 Bg7 4. Nc3 Nc6 5. a3 a6 6. Rb1 Rb8 7. b4 cxb4 8. axb4 b5 9. cxb5 axb5** (see diagram). Here Rowson remarks,

> Both sides want to push their d-pawn and play Bf4/...Bf5, but White has to go first so Black gets to play ...d5 before White can play d4. This doesn't matter much, but it already points to the challenge that White faces here; his most natural continuations allow Black to play the moves he wants to. I would therefore say that White is in 'Zugzwang Lite' and that he remains in this state for several moves.

The game continued **10\. Nf3 d5 11. d4 Nf6 12. Bf4 Rb6 13. 0-0 Bf5 14. Rb3 0-0 15. Ne5 Ne4 16. h3 h5!? 17. Kh2**. The position is still almost symmetrical, and White can find nothing useful to do with his extra move. Rowson whimsically suggests 17.h4!?, forcing Black to be the one to break the symmetry. **17... Re8!** Rowson notes that this is a useful waiting move, covering e7, which needs protection in some lines, and possibly supporting an eventual ...e5 (as Black in fact played on his 22nd move). White cannot copy it, since after 18.Re1? Nxf2 Black would win a [pawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pawn_(chess) "Pawn (chess)"). After **18\. Be3?! Nxe5! 19. dxe5 Rc6!** Black seized the initiative and went on to win in 14 more moves.

Another instance of _Zugzwang Lite_ occurred in [Lajos Portisch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lajos_Portisch "Lajos Portisch") versus [Mikhail Tal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Tal "Mikhail Tal"), [Candidates Match](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candidates_Match "Candidates Match") 1965, again from the [Symmetrical Variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Opening#Common_responses "English Opening") of the [English Opening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Opening "English Opening"), after **1\. Nf3 c5 2. c4 Nc6 3. Nc3 Nf6 4. g3 g6 5. Bg2 Bg7 6. 0-0 0-0 7. d3 a6 8. a3 Rb8 9. Rb1 b5 10. cxb5 axb5 11. b4 cxb4 12. axb4 d6 13. Bd2 Bd7** (see diagram). Soltis wrote, "It's ridiculous to think Black's position is better. But Mikhail Tal said it is easier to play. By moving second he gets to see White's move and then decide whether to match it."[\[62\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-Soltis2008-62)**14\. Qc1** Here, Soltis wrote that Black could maintain equality by keeping the symmetry: 14...Qc8 15.Bh6 Bh3. Instead, he plays to prove that White's queen is misplaced by breaking the symmetry. **14... Rc8! 15. Bh6 Nd4!** Threatening 15...Nxe2+. **16\. Nxd4 Bxh6 17. Qxh6 Rxc3 18. Qd2 Qc7 19. Rfc1 Rc8** Although the pawn structure is still symmetrical, Black's control of the [c-file](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#file "Glossary of chess") gives him the advantage.[\[62\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-Soltis2008-62) Black ultimately reached an endgame two pawns up, but White managed to hold a draw in 83 moves.[\[63\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugzwang#cite_note-63) See [First-move advantage in chess#Symmetrical openings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-move_advantage_in_chess#Symmetrical_openings "First-move advantage in chess") for more details.
